For much of the past year, Refractions has been a Founding Label member of [Subvert](https://subvert.fm/), a cooperative that aims to create a collectively owned Bandcamp successor. During this period, we have engaged with other members in discussing the music industry as it is today, where it might go, and how we might influence its direction. Back in January we posed a sharp question to cooperative members, in the interest of understanding how listeners of today consume music:

> **Why Should I Pay For Music?**
>
> *Hi all, bit of a deliberately controversial title here!*
>
> *I want to know, do you pay for music, and if so in what formats and why? Is it purely a moral decision (eg. I should support the artists I listen to), is there a functional reason (eg. being a DJ), do you value physical ownership (eg. records)?*
>
> *If you don’t directly purchase music, do you indirectly spend money on music (eg. streaming subscription, live events, merch)?*
>
> *If you don’t spend any money on music at all, do you still consume it (eg. via piracy or free platforms like YouTube)? Would you spend money on music if there was a compelling value proposition? What might that be?*
>
> *I’m interested in finding ways to create value propositions for music that work in the industry of the 21st century. How do we find robust ways to monetise music in a post-scarcity landscape that don’t just rely on emotional or moral motivations? Has the digital age ended the era of recorded music monetisation, or has it opened up even greater possibilities?*
>
> *Curious to hear what people think!*

This question received 60 responses, and opened up multiple conversations among members about how they consume music and where they perceive value to lie. After reading through the thoughts of the community, a number of trends and interesting perspectives came to light. This series of Insights, Why Should I Pay For Music?, will look at some of the themes discussed in the responses, and consider how these could influence how we create and understand value in the post-scarcity music industry of the 21st century.

Part 1 of this series, Raised on Piracy, is an examination of the industry landscape I personally grew up in, and how my relationship with music began and continues to this day. The trigger for this line of thinking came from one member (I will be keeping responses anonymous to respect the private nature of the forum) who said the following:

> *being a kid with no money i did do a bit of ‘downloading’ when i wanted to put things on my mp3 player.*

As a former kid with no money myself, I have done my fair share of ‘downloading’. My first discovery of electronic music, the genre I have now dedicated my career to, was fuelled by YouTube rips of Deadmau5 and Skrillex loaded onto my iPod Nano. I grew up in the era just before the ubiquity of smartphones and iPads. The CD still ruled my parents’ listening habits, and the idea of paying a subscription to stream music would have still been in its infancy. Consequently, my music discovery as a child was driven by piracy. I would spend hours downloading from slightly dodgy websites (at this point I had moved on from YouTube rips) and loading up my iPod with bass music.

The reason I explain this is not to excuse my piracy, but to understand how my consumption habits of music were influenced by the world I found myself in at the time. The irony of it all is that it is this piracy that led me to this career I am now in: selling music. To this day I still sit with a tension when it comes to paying for music — a discomfort with something that feels unnatural to me. I realise that there is an entire generation of people who, like myself, grew up pirating music, and who have a very different sense of the value of recorded music compared to those who grew up on physical media.

When I consider the differences between the process of music acquisition for those of my generation and of my parents’, two key aspects stand out:

**Availability**

The advent of the internet revolutionised how information is shared. The barriers to distribution and receipt were blown apart, with digital files capable of immediate and costless transmission to anyone with a computer, anywhere in the world. Unlike physical media, digital media can be duplicated endlessly, with no manufacturing overheads and bit-perfect accuracy. This is the media landscape I grew up in. My parents, however, grew up in a completely different world. Recorded music was a finite resource, which required effort, and money, to acquire. Where for me the barrier to acquisition was a type into a search bar and a click to download, for them it was a trip into town, a chance on the stock of the record shop, and an inescapable exchange of money. Could it be that the expectation I grew up with, that the music I wanted I could immediately acquire at no cost, reduced the perceived value I placed on said music?

**Experience**

For those of my parents’ generation, the trip to the record shop was itself part of the value of recorded music. Flicking through bins, previewing a record, sharing a space and an experience with others. In many ways, the value of recorded music began before it was ever listened to. Whilst the internet transformed the range of music available to the listener, it did so for an audience of one, and removed the charm and the friction that gave the acquisition experience of old value. The process of acquiring digital music is sterile and insular, an experience that is designed to be as frictionless as possible. When an entire element of the value of recorded music is removed, is it any surprise that the perceived value lessens as a result?

I don’t write this to blame the digital transformation of the music acquisition process for how I perceive value in recorded music today, but to understand how changing experiences with music can affect value perception. If removing the pleasure, the friction and the intentionality of the acquisition process reduces the value we place on recorded music, what can we add that may shift the dial the other way?

Over the past few years, an interesting trend has emerged. Gen Z, a generation that has never known a world without on-demand digital content, are increasingly resisting what is often seen as the inevitable slide towards a world of algorithmically optimised, infinite content consumption. Music streaming, TikTok and AI have all turned the process of listening to music into something passive, locked away behind a device designed to suck attention into a black hole. The freedom to stream anything and everything has become the mechanism for algorithmic control and attention capture, and many in Gen Z are now making the conscious decision to take back control over their listening habits. This return to physical, to friction and limitations, represents a return to intentionality.

In Part 2 I will be discussing the return of intentionality to music consumption in the modern world, and how this may serve as one mechanism to return the lost value to recorded music.

In Part 1 of this series I introduced the concept of *The Access Model*; a view of the music industry that sees the economic value of music rooted in access, not inherent to the music itself. In this article I will be applying *The Access Model* to what are often considered the two largest threats to the contemporary music industry – Streaming and AI – and assessing the reality of such fears when viewed through this lens.

#### **Sterile Algorithms**

In 2025, Luminate's Year-End Report documented that 106,000 tracks were delivered each day to DSPs. Over the span of one year, 38.6M new tracks became available to stream, representing an 18% catalogue growth from 2024. 88% of the available catalogue achieved 1000 streams or less in 2025, meaning that only 12% of tracks crossed the royalty payment threshold currently employed by Spotify.

What do all of these numbers tell us? In plain English, the success of digital distribution in lowering the barrier to entry to the music industry has also lowered the threshold for the music being distributed. Where once label execs and A&R acted as the filter for the music that would be released to the world, the only thing that now stands in the way is the sign-up form on a music distributor's website.

With DSPs so waterlogged with low quality music, simply giving subscribers the keys to the door of the catalogue and leaving them to it would see the user wading through the boggy marshes of the 88% to find something worth listening to. As a result, DSPs have developed a number of ways to guide users to the music that is actually worth their time, including curated playlists, charts and algorithms.

Algorithms exist largely as black boxes within their host platforms; reading, ranking and weighing metrics from numerous signals and attributes to decide what music to surface when. Whilst we can't see the inner workings of these algorithms, we can glean from the outputs that they generally prioritise popular artists and trending tracks over lesser-known music.

At first glance, algorithms may seem a perfectly good solution to the problem of the glut of music that is constantly pumped into DSPs – automatically separating the wheat from the chaff and ensuring that the listener doesn't get lost in the mangled marshes of the 88%. But an algorithm that has been optimised to surface popular artists and trending music is also one that has been programmed to ignore music that pushes boundaries, takes risks and challenges the musical status quo. The end result of this is that algorithms sterilise music discovery, swapping the 'dirty' human elements of musical experimentation and intuition with a 'clean' logic engine that bases its recommendations entirely on what has already been.

Now this is not to say that there is no place for a lowest common denominator 'tastemaker' of this sort. In the days of yore, major label marketing, pop charts and music television channels played the role of surfacing homogenised music based on metrics and logic. Algorithms have largely usurped these in the modern industry, swapping human calculations for computer, however the fundamental limitations of algorithms mean that they are unable to replace the tastemakers that harness the irreplicable aspects of human nature.

An algorithm that functions based on the logic of what has already been is unable to produce something that has not already come. A world where music is driven entirely by algorithms is a world where music is not driven at all. There would be no new genres, no artists who push the bounds of what already exists, no music that challenges; a sterile industry incapable of reproduction. This is where applying *The Access Model* serves to assuage fears of the all-powerful algorithm dictating the music industry of the future. For those who simply want access to lowest common denominator music, the algorithm will serve the same role that charts and music TV played previously, however for those who want access to music beyond this, the algorithm offers them nothing of value.

#### **AI Slop**

In assessing algorithms through the lens of *The Access Model*, the similarities they share with generative AI in regard to their function in the music industry become strikingly clear. The fears of many in the industry, that AI generated music will replace artist created music, are based on the presupposition that people want access to AI generated music. But if we take the arguments made in the previous section and transpose them to here, what do we see?

1. **The 88%** — As previously discussed, 88% of all music available on streaming platforms has not crossed the 1000 play threshold. This quagmire of low-quality music is vast and treacherous, but what does it matter if AI generated music further floods a swamp that most don't want to visit anyway?


2. **Black boxes and 'clean' logic** — Strip back the marketing, hype and apocalyptic claims around AI, and we realise that generative AI models are still nothing more than opaque prediction engines that simply analyse inputs and deliver the most probable output in response. These suffer the same weaknesses as algorithms, where 'clean' logic is incapable of replacing the 'dirty' elements of the human.


3. **Incapacity for reproduction** — The inherent nature of prediction engines, trained on what has already been, means that they are unable to produce something that has not already come. Just as a species would go extinct if reproduction became impossible, a source of music incapable of evolution would quickly find its output stale and unpalatable.

Just as before, there will remain people who want access to nothing more than lowest common denominator music, who listen simply for something to listen to. This section of the music industry is the most at risk from AI, but those who are happy listening to music generated by a prediction machine and then filtered through a logic engine are those who only place a low value on access to music in the first place.

In part 1 of this series, we discussed the diminishing value placed on recorded music by listeners of the modern world, and how a reduction of intentionality in the music acquisition processes of today may play a part in driving this trend. Whilst intentionality is often framed as an inevitable victim of the age of algorithms, signs of resistance remain. Even deep inside the den of automaticity that is the streaming platform, the humble user-generated playlist endures as a beacon of active engagement and personal investment with music. To quote a paper from the University of Edinburgh:

> *Users imbue intangible streaming music with personal significance and symbolic value through careful curation and organisation.*

Even with the forces of the modern recorded music landscape stacked against it, the embers of intentionality continue to burn. This leads us to the question of where the drivers of intentionality truly reside. Perhaps intentionality was not a necessity born of the inherent constraints on supply imposed by physical media, but instead an element of our relationship with music that had found a perfect partner in the technological limitations of the time? When we say that physical media made us value music more, are we missing the point that physical media was but a conduit of intentionality, and that the physical aspect was no more valuable than any other conduit that could have channelled intentionality to the same degree?

Returning to the Subvert thread that inspired this series, we find a pertinent contribution:

> *The more we have an active relationship with the music we buy, the more we value it — and the more likely we are to actually buy it.*

In a world where music has become detached from the economics of the physical medium it was distributed on, and where vastly increased supply and ease of access have changed the nexus of value, new conduits through which we channel intentionality must be forged. Perhaps it is this active relationship with music that now holds one of the keys to determining the intentionality, and thus the worth that the listener places on recorded music.

Over the past few years, a curious and somewhat counterintuitive return to intentionality has begun to gather pace in the world of music consumption. MP3 players, once confined to the dustbin of history alongside such antiquities as the steam engine and feudalism, have seen a renewal of interest amongst Gen Z. Those who grew up with the world in the palm of their hands are increasingly choosing the path of more resistance, a conscious deviation from and rejection of the technology that promised to revolutionise how we consume music. But why?

There are three key elements to the MP3 player experience that I believe are driving this uptake:

**A limited library is an intentional one**

In a world of limitless access and the relentless churn of the release cycle, our relationships with individual pieces of music become ever more fleeting. Last week’s New Music Friday becomes yesterday’s jam once the next rolls around, and even music that connected gets quickly usurped by the novelty of something new.

By choosing a means of listening to music with fundamental limits – offline, limited storage, no pre-existing library – the user is forced to consider every piece of music that they load onto the device. Each track needs to earn its place and prove its value, the time and expense of acquisition filtering out those which fall short. In some ways, the MP3 player is the user-generated playlist to the extreme: users carefully curate a collection of music which holds to them personal significance.

The process of curating an intentionally limited library, one in which every track must prove it belongs, develops and necessitates a deeply active and engaged relationship with music. It slows the pace of consumption, encouraging the listener to sit for longer and to engage more deeply with a body of work.

**A listening experience with friction is an intentional one**

In theory, the allure of unlimited access to the entirety of the world’s musical output at the tap of a screen is undeniable. No longer am I limited to what my local record store has in stock, or what the DJs and radio pluggers decide to play on the airwaves. I have a world of music at my hands, and it is my oyster. But in practice, such availability rarely leads to organic discovery of new music. Perhaps it’s option paralysis, perhaps it’s the Netflix problem of ‘water, water everywhere, but not a drop to drink’, but unlimited access becomes less appealing when algorithms ultimately drive discovery.

In a previous Insight, we discussed the fundamental properties of algorithms that lead to bland and homogenised music discovery, with consumption becoming the inevitable subsequent victim. By adding friction back to the discovery and consumption process - not just by removing algorithms, but by adding the necessity of downloading, syncing, and plugging in headphones to a different device entirely – intentionality is added back to the action of listening. Listening shifts from a background operation to one closer to the foreground - one that takes active, conscious engagement to facilitate.

**An owned music library is an intentional one**

The nature of any relationship is defined by its terms. The permissions, constraints and obligations conferred on each party dictate the bounds within which interaction occurs. A citizen's relationship with the state differs fundamentally from that of a non-citizen; the owner of a property holds a categorically different set of rights over it than a visitor or tenant.

In the era of music streaming, the listener’s relationship with the music they listen to has seen a fundamental change in this regard. Where once a music library consisted of music owned by the curator, a library based on music streaming is leased to the listener, one stream at a time. A music library which exists only at the permission of a third-party, and which could be revoked at any time, is one that embodies a far more transient and ethereal nature, and one which may in fact encourage emotional hedging. With music streaming, I could wake up tomorrow and find that my music library has completely disappeared, and so I may subconsciously limit my investment in and engagement with the music I listen to as a primal act of emotional self-protection.

By choosing to own the music that constitutes my library, and to store and play it back from my own hardware, I choose a mode of music consumption that gives me the security to emotionally engage with my library – fully, and on my own terms.

---

Whilst we can undoubtedly credit some of the resurgence of the MP3 player to the current Y2K aesthetic revival, this revival has not driven a resurgence of other technologies from the era. People are not ditching their broadband for dial-up or installing a stand-alone sat-nav in their car, suggesting there is something deeper than simply nostalgia at play. Beyond the aesthetics of the era, the real revival I believe the MP3 player represents is that of intentionality. Just as physical media was the conduit for intentionality in the 20th century, the MP3 player is increasingly finding itself filling that role today. The MP3 player may well fade again as the wheel of fashion revolves once more, but the desire for intentionality will remain. The question then is, how do we build a lasting conduit of intentionality for the 21st century?
